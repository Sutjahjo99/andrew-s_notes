Chapter 1: Mole Balance


1.1 Rate of Reaction

The rate of reaction tells us how fast or slow 1-mole of a chemical species is being consumed in a chemical reaction. Hence the rate of disappearance of chemical species j will be denoted by the symbol \( -r_j \). Now, given the chemical reaction:

\(A + B \rightarrow C + D\)

We can denote the rate of disappearance of species A and B as \( -r_A \) and \( -r_B \) respectively as they are being consumed to synthesize C and D. Whereas species C and D are currently being formed, therefore, their respective rate of formation may be denoted by \( r_C \) and \( r_D \). There are several nuances that needs to be noted about the rate laws. The rate law:

  • is an algebraic equation.
  • is dependent ONLY on the properties of the reacting materials and surrounding conditions.
  • is NOT dependent on the type of reactor used to carry out the reaction.
In addition to the rate of formation \( r_j \) and the rate of disappearance \( -r_j \), there is a similar rate notation: \( -r_{j}' \), which denotes the rate law of a heterogeneous reaction. A heterogeneous reaction is a chemical reaction that involves more than one phase of matter - an example of this would be the use of a solid catalyst in a solution to speed up a reaction.



The rate law has a relationship with the concentration of the participating chemical species in a chemical reaction. Let's take a gander at another example:

\(A \rightarrow products\)

As such, here are some examples of what the rate law of A may look like:

  • \( -r_A = kC_A \)
  • \( -r_A = kC_A^2 \)
  • \( -r_A = \frac{k_1C_A}{1 + k_2C_A} \)
depending on its relationship with the concentration of chemical species A, where k is the rate constant and \(C_A\) is the concentration of A.

Quick Notes

Standard units
  • \( r_j \) = \( \frac{moles}{volume \cdot time} \)
  • \( r_j' \) = \( \frac{moles}{time \cdot mass_{catalyst}} \)
